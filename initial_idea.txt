This is a promising paper idea, and there is a real gap here. Recent work has studied verbalized confidence, calibration, abstention, semantic uncertainty, and CoT faithfulness, but the combination you want to test is still underexplored: when uncertainty appears during reasoning, whether it is surfaced or suppressed in the final answer, and how that differs between open models with visible traces and production models that expose only summaries or partial reasoning signals.  ￼

My strongest recommendation is to frame the project around uncertainty transmission across the reasoning pipeline. In other words, measure the path from internal or exposed reasoning signal to user-facing answer. That framing connects safety, interpretability, and calibration, and it avoids making the paper depend on proving that all visible CoT is fully faithful. The faithfulness literature already shows that CoT can be only partially reflective of true internal computation, especially as models get stronger, so your paper can be careful and still novel by studying exposed reasoning traces as behavioral evidence rather than as ground truth cognition.  ￼

A good literature review can be organized into five clusters.

First, there is the uncertainty and calibration line. The 2025 survey on uncertainty quantification and confidence calibration is useful as a map of the space. Earlier empirical work on eliciting confidence from LLMs showed that black-box verbalized confidence can be informative, but prompt format matters a lot. More recent work on verbalized confidence scores argues that such scores can be reasonably calibrated under some prompting schemes.  ￼

Second, there is the natural-language uncertainty expression line. Work on linguistic calibration of long-form generations studies whether models communicate uncertainty in a way that lets users make calibrated downstream judgments. Related work on models expressing uncertainty or verbalized semantic confidence is highly relevant if you want to code phrases like “likely,” “I might be mistaken,” or “I am not sure” in final outputs.  ￼

Third, there is the CoT-specific uncertainty line. A particularly relevant recent paper argues that confidence calibration for CoT reasoning had been largely unexplored, and shows that confidence supervision can induce longer, more self-checking responses on low-confidence items. That is very close to your idea because it suggests that uncertainty may change not only what models say, but also how reasoning unfolds. CoT-UQ is also relevant because it explicitly tries to estimate response-level uncertainty from reasoning steps.  ￼

Fourth, there is the faithfulness and hidden-computation line. “Measuring Faithfulness in Chain-of-Thought Reasoning” and later work like FUR both show that visible reasoning is not always a faithful window into the model’s actual decision process. Work on hidden computations likewise suggests that models may carry out meaningful internal computation even when visible reasoning is altered or compressed. For your paper, this means you should avoid claims like “the CoT is the model’s true uncertainty state” and instead speak about “exposed reasoning traces” or “available reasoning artifacts.”  ￼

Fifth, there is the abstention and self-knowledge line. SelfAware, Unknown-Bench, UBench, and AbstentionBench all matter because they focus on whether models know when they do not know, and whether they abstain on unanswerable or underspecified questions. Those are excellent sources for part of your task suite because they create genuine uncertainty, not just hard reasoning.  ￼

Given that background, I would make your central research questions something like this. When models generate exposed reasoning traces, how often do those traces contain uncertainty markers. Where in the trace do those markers appear. Does uncertainty in the trace predict error, abstention, correction, or hedging in the final answer. And when a model has access to a reasoning mode but exposes only summaries, how much uncertainty survives compression from reasoning artifact to final answer. Those questions are novel while staying implementable.  ￼

A simple, publishable methodology would use a two-stratum model set.

One stratum should be open or locally run models where you can get a visible reasoning trace. Ollama now supports “thinking” output for several reasoning-capable models, including Qwen 3, GPT-OSS, DeepSeek-v3.1, and DeepSeek R1, and the API separates message.thinking from final message.content.  ￼

The other stratum should be production APIs where you can get only a reasoning summary or provider-specific reasoning artifact. OpenAI reasoning models do not expose raw reasoning tokens but can return a reasoning summary when you opt in. Gemini thinking models can return thought summaries with includeThoughts=true. Anthropic’s extended thinking returns a summary of full thinking for Claude 4 models when enabled. That means your comparison will not be raw-CoT versus raw-CoT for every provider. Instead, it becomes a comparison of exposed reasoning artifacts across transparency regimes, which is itself an interesting contribution.  ￼

I would keep the first paper deliberately simple and use around 6 to 8 models. For example, two or three Ollama models with visible thinking, one OpenAI reasoning model with summaries, one Claude model with extended thinking summaries, and one Gemini thinking model with thought summaries. That is enough heterogeneity for a strong paper without turning it into an infrastructure project.  ￼

For tasks, I would not rely on only GSM8K-style solvable reasoning. You want a mix of difficulty-driven uncertainty and epistemic uncertainty. A strong simple design is three buckets. Bucket one is answerable reasoning tasks such as GSM8K, MATH-style subsets, or multi-step QA. Bucket two is unanswerable, underspecified, or false-premise questions from datasets like SelfAware, Unknown-Bench, and AbstentionBench. Bucket three is factual QA where semantic uncertainty can be estimated by repeated sampling. This mix lets you ask whether uncertainty expressions behave differently for “hard but answerable” versus “should abstain” items.  ￼

I would aim for about 500 to 1,500 prompts total in the first pass, balanced across those buckets. That is enough for mixed-effects modeling and error analysis, and still manageable with API costs. If you sample multiple generations per prompt for a subset, you can estimate semantic entropy or answer dispersion without exploding budget. The semantic uncertainty literature is a good basis for that repeated-sampling component.  ￼

Your prompt design should be standardized and minimal. Use one main prompt template across all models. Ask the model to think step by step if supported by the provider, then provide a final answer in a strict output schema. I would have the final answer schema include four fields: final_answer, abstain as yes or no, confidence_0_100, and brief_justification. The important thing is that uncertainty in the final answer is not only inferred from wording but also directly elicited numerically. The verbalized-confidence literature suggests prompt method matters a lot, so keep the elicitation format fixed across models.  ￼

For the reasoning artifact, store whatever the provider gives you as a separate field. For Ollama, save the full thinking trace and final content separately. For OpenAI, save the reasoning summary and final answer separately. For Gemini, save thought-summary parts separately from answer parts. For Anthropic, save the summarized thinking block separately from the text block. This separation is crucial because your core dependent variable is transmission from reasoning artifact to final answer.  ￼

For annotation, I would avoid trying to “discover uncertainty” with only a black-box classifier. Instead, build a transparent feature set first, then optionally add an LLM judge. A simple coding scheme for reasoning artifacts and final answers would include hedging words, explicit uncertainty statements, self-corrections, alternative hypotheses, requests for missing information, defeaters such as “unless,” and abstentions. Also code position in the trace by splitting it into normalized quartiles or deciles and marking where uncertainty first appears. That directly answers your “where does it happen in CoT” question.

A strong operationalization would define these variables per response. Whether uncertainty appears in the reasoning artifact. Whether uncertainty appears in the final answer. The first position where uncertainty appears. The count or rate of uncertainty markers per 100 tokens. Whether the reasoning artifact contains a revision event, such as “wait,” “actually,” or “let me reconsider.” Whether the final answer is assertive, hedged, or abstaining. Whether the answer is correct. Whether the question is truly answerable. And the model’s self-reported confidence. Those variables are simple enough to compute and strong enough to support publishable analysis.

For scoring correctness, use exact match where possible for math or multiple choice, and a lightweight semantic scorer for open-ended QA. For answerability and abstention tasks, define success carefully. On unanswerable items, success is appropriate abstention or explicit acknowledgment of missing information. On answerable items, success is correctness without unnecessary abstention. This follows the abstention literature more closely than a generic accuracy metric.  ￼

For uncertainty estimation beyond lexical markers, add one repeated-sampling baseline. For a subset of prompts, generate perhaps 5 to 10 samples per model at fixed temperature and compute answer dispersion or semantic entropy. That gives you an external behavioral uncertainty signal to compare against visible uncertainty in the reasoning artifact and final answer. Semantic uncertainty work is the right starting point here because it handles semantically equivalent answers rather than just string mismatch.  ￼

The cleanest statistical analysis would have three layers.

At the descriptive layer, report uncertainty rate in reasoning artifacts, uncertainty rate in final answers, transmission rate from reasoning to final answer, and suppression rate where uncertainty appears in reasoning but not in final output. Break these out by model and task type.

At the predictive layer, fit mixed-effects logistic regressions. One model predicts correctness from reasoning uncertainty, final-answer uncertainty, self-reported confidence, task type, and provider type, with random intercepts for question and model. Another predicts final-answer hedging or abstention from reasoning uncertainty features and task type. A third predicts suppression, defined as uncertainty in reasoning artifact but not final output.

At the temporal layer, model first-uncertainty-position as a predictor. The hypothesis here is that earlier uncertainty signals may be associated with either better self-correction or worse final accuracy, depending on task type. That is a substantive result reviewers will find interesting because it moves beyond simple presence versus absence.

Your core hypotheses could be these. H1, uncertainty markers in exposed reasoning artifacts increase on difficult and unanswerable items. H2, visible uncertainty in reasoning predicts lower raw accuracy but better-calibrated abstention. H3, production systems will show more suppression of uncertainty from reasoning artifact to final answer than open models with raw traces, partly because some providers expose summaries rather than full traces. H4, earlier uncertainty onset and self-correction cues in reasoning traces predict better final calibration than late uncertainty or no uncertainty. H5, self-reported numeric confidence is informative but less reliable than behavioral trace features unless elicited with a good prompt format.  ￼

The biggest methodological risk is faithfulness. Reviewers may say visible CoT is not the true internal state. You should preempt that directly. Say that your paper studies exposed reasoning artifacts and their relationship to user-facing outputs, not hidden cognition itself. Then cite the faithfulness papers and treat the question as behavioral and interface-level safety. That framing is both accurate and defensible.  ￼

The second risk is provider asymmetry. OpenAI gives summaries, not raw reasoning tokens. Gemini exposes thought summaries. Anthropic exposes summarized thinking in current extended-thinking docs. That means direct apples-to-apples CoT comparison is impossible. But that does not sink the paper. It suggests a stronger design where “artifact type” is a factor with levels like raw trace and summarized trace. In practice, you can still compare whether uncertainty present in the available artifact reaches the final answer.  ￼

The third risk is overengineering. You do not need representation probing or hidden-state classifiers in paper one. Work like “The Internal State of an LLM Knows When It’s Lying” shows that internal activations can be predictive, but that would complicate the study a lot. Keep that as future work unless you decide to do a smaller open-model-only mechanistic appendix.  ￼

A very practical implementation stack would be Python with one unified response schema. Use provider wrappers that all save prompt_id, model, provider, task_bucket, question_text, reasoning_artifact, artifact_type, final_answer, abstain, confidence_0_100, raw_text, latency, and metadata. For local models, Ollama is especially convenient because it already separates thinking from answer at the API level.  ￼

If you want the simplest credible paper, I would narrow the scope to this title-level idea. From Thought to Answer: How LLMs Express and Suppress Uncertainty Across Reasoning Traces and Final Outputs. That gives you one strong contribution, one manageable dataset, and a methodology you can actually run with APIs and Ollama.

A tight experimental plan for the next two weeks would be this. First, finalize a task suite of about 600 prompts split across answerable reasoning, unanswerable questions, and factual QA. Second, build a collector that stores reasoning artifact and answer separately for 6 models. Third, hand-label 100 to 150 samples to validate your uncertainty taxonomy. Fourth, run the full collection with one sample per prompt plus a repeated-sampling subset. Fifth, fit mixed-effects models and do targeted error analysis on suppression cases.
